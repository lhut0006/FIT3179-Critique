This dashboard presents visual insights into Twitter discussions surrounding the hashtags #Trump and #Biden during the 2020 American presidential election.

Election Overview

This visualisation depicts the number of global tweets containing the hashtags #Biden and #Trump in the lead-up to and just after the 2020 U.S. Presidential election. Two key events are marked: the Second Presidential Debate on October 22, 2020, and Election Day on November 3, 2020.

There is a notable spike in both hashtags following the Second Presidential Debate, where the #Trump hashtag slightly leads in number of tweets. However, as the end of the election approaches, the #Biden hashtag begins to surpass #Trump, with a significant increase around Election Day itself, peaking at around 150,000 tweets.

This trend suggests that discussions about Biden gained more traction globally as the election date neared, with tweet activity for both candidates increasing but #Biden gaining more momentum as results were announced.

Common Discourse

The word cloud visualises the most frequent words in tweets related to #Trump and #Biden, highlighting the prominence of both political figures in public discourse. Larger words such as "trump," "biden," "joebiden," and "realdonaldtrump" dominate the visualisation, indicating the frequent mentions of these terms. Words like "election," "president," and "vote" suggest a focus on the election and the broader political environment.

In addition to English terms, there are a variety of words in other languages like "es," "de," and "una," showing the international reach and engagement in discussions surrounding these political figures. The overall focus clearly revolves around the two key political figures, their campaigns, and related political events or sentiments.

Hourly Breakdown

The plot depicts user engagement by hour for tweets containing the hashtags #Biden and #Trump. Each data point represents the average engagement levels throughout the day, with the blue circles indicating #Biden tweets and the orange circles representing #Trump tweets.

The overall trend suggests that engagement levels for both hashtags tend to fluctuate, with some peaks observed around certain hours. Notably, the engagement for #Biden appears to be slightly higher during the early morning hours, while #Trump's engagement is relatively consistent but slightly lower.

A general trend of engagement around hours 15-18 is visible in the data for #Biden, where these hours coincide with the candidate being elected, causing a spike in engagement on Twitter around the globe.

Sentiment analysis of tweets measures the emotions expressed in social media posts to understand public opinion. By examining the language used, we can classify tweets as positive, negative, or neutral. This helps us uncover how people feel about specific topics or events.

From this graph, we can infer that tweets about Biden tend to carry a more positive sentiment, whereas tweets about Trump tend to have a more negative sentiment throughout the day. These insights suggest a stark contrast in how users engaged with the two candidates on Twitter during the presidential election. The sudden spike in sentiment for Biden around 3pm coincides with the hours after the candidate was announced as president.

Tweets Around the World

This choropleth map visualises the global engagement with the hashtags #Trump and #Biden on Twitter during the 2020 U.S. presidential election. The colours represent an Engagement Index, using min-max normalisation to show the relative number of tweets per capita across countries.

Unsurprisingly, North America, particularly the United States and Canada, show the highest levels of engagement. Other regions, such as Australia and parts of Europe, also demonstrate significant interest in the election despite being geographically isolated from the United States.

In contrast, large areas in Africa, South America, and parts of Asia show minimal engagement, highlighting how attention to the U.S. election varied globally with significant engagement from specific English speaking regions.
